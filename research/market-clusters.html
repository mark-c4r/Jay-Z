Market & peer-cluster map

Surface-level scan. Desk research, not field research — where I’m guessing vs. where I have public evidence is flagged per block. All five peers below have been studied from their public sites, YouTube channels, and published interviews; I’ve never spoken to any of them.

Ceranna Photography

Observed

Dual-market portfolio — Italy + Czech Republic — with adventure-elopement as the whole site frame, not a sub-category within a broader wedding gallery. Portfolio indexed by story and couple rather than venue or season. Bilingual content track is visible in the site architecture (CZ and EN versions of individual stories).

Inferred

The dual-language commitment looks deliberate, not incidental. Bilingual SEO is probably being pursued on purpose. The adventure-elopement lane is narrow enough to own from two different geographies at once.

What it may mean for you

The structural bet — pick one narrow lane, commit to both languages on purpose — transfers directly, even though your lane is different (record + 45 peaks + Scandinavian adventure, not pure elopement). The bilingual edge Ceranna has in wedding is the same bilingual edge you already have sitting unused in 91 Czech podcast episodes.

First low-risk experiment

Pick one subject (one peak, one route, one trip) and publish a post about it in both CZ and EN in the same week. See how Google handles dual-language indexation, where referral traffic lands, and which language converts to inquiries at a better rate.

Avvagraphy

Observed

Prague-wedding-duo shape. Positioning is traditional, package-centric, less of the adventure frame. Site reads as conventional Prague market — a useful reference for the classical Prague category, not a direct competitor in the adventure-elopement lane.

Inferred

Different market entirely. The overlap is geographic (Prague) but not segmental (adventure elopement). Their buyers and yours probably don’t meet on Google for the same searches.

What it may mean for you

A possible secondary lane if you ever want to keep one foot in traditional Prague wedding work, but not the primary market to study. Useful as a reminder of the other end of the same city: the classical-Prague brand you’re not trying to be.

First low-risk experiment

None proposed. Different niche, different buyer — keep as a reference point for where you’re not positioning, not as a model to emulate.

2 Brides — Isabelle Hesselberg

Observed

Stockholm-based. Editorial wedding shape — premium, portfolio-dominant, one-lane-and-doubles-down. City-hall elopements is the specific lane she’s committed to. Site structure follows that commitment: the whole brand reads as a single answer to a single kind of couple.

Inferred

The pattern across the wider Swedish scene you’re in: most peers pick exactly one lane and stay. The premium-editorial end is well covered; the adventure-elopement end is noticeably thinner. Lane-commitment seems to be the rule, not the exception.

What it may mean for you

Adventure-elopement in and around Stockholm is underserved relative to city-hall editorial. Your record + summit angle is orthogonal to the premium-editorial lane — it’s not a direct compete, but it’s the adjacent segment with real demand and few committed operators.

First low-risk experiment

Publish one Stockholm-vicinity adventure-elopement post with the record anchor explicit in the opening paragraph. See whether it lands with the editorial-wedding audience already searching Swedish names, or pulls in a different reader entirely.

Honest Guide

Observed

Two-host Prague YouTube channel run by Janek Rubeš and Honza Mikulka. Not a competitor — tourism/travel domain, not wedding or adventure. The authenticity contract is the brand: local expert protects visitors from tourist traps, warm humor, earned point of view. Long-running, Czech-origin, English-speaking.

Inferred

Authenticity-first brands scale slowly at first, then compound. The POV is the moat: another Prague guide could copy every individual recommendation and still not be Honest Guide. The thing that compounds is the credibility of the voice.

What it may mean for you

“Honest Guide through the Czech / European / Scandinavian mountains” is a viable brand lens — not a tagline to copy, a decision filter for what to publish and how. The record + Koruna Evropy + Stockholm + bilingual + actual mountaineering chops are exactly what earns the right to use “honest” in that context.

First low-risk experiment

One first-person video in the Honest Guide format: no-BS, earned POV, one route or one peak, warm humor, what the guidebooks don’t tell you. See whether the voice travels — and whether the record anchor lands better in video than in text.

Paul Zizka

Observed

Banff, Canada. Fifteen years into a career built from one specific place and compounded. Eight books. Commercial clients. A 23,000-member community platform built around the same mountain region. Self-taught path. Family-first geography — he built the brand around staying where his kids grew up.

Inferred

The shape of the stack works. The timeline is long — most of the visible output came after year five. The late years outperform the early years by a big margin because the archive itself becomes an asset that new work multiplies off of.

What it may mean for you

Forty-five European peaks plus Scandinavia is structurally the same bet — one specific, under-documented geographic range that compounds over decades. Zizka isn’t a peer; he’s a template. The record plus the peaks plus the Stockholm base is your version of “one place, compounded.”

First low-risk experiment

Pick one peak that could anchor a five-year micro-archive — one that deserves a book, a set of prints, a guide partnership, and a handful of trip reports over time. Everything else stays opportunistic. One peak, five years, see how the archive compounds.
