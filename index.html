AI right now — what Jeff & Jack covered

Hi Jay Z,

This is the video that makes the case for Cowork over Chat. Three differences up front: Cowork touches your local files (no 20-file limit), does the work instead of telling you how, and takes outcome-first prompts (“give me an Excel of these 100 receipts”) instead of step-by-step tasks.

The meat is seven capabilities stacked on each other. Local files + persistent memory (stored as Claude.md / memory.md on your disk) are the foundation. On top: connectors (Gmail/Notion/Drive), skills (reusable mini-apps you build by teaching once then asking Cowork to codify), and projects that bundle everything.

The browser extension is rough. But scheduled tasks — a 6am inbox triage, fully automated — actually work, because all seven capabilities stack under them.

The one thing to keep: Cowork isn’t a smarter chatbot. It’s a co-worker that writes to your filesystem and remembers what you told it.

What people are actually building — Jesse Genet

Two interviews, same person. The a16z one is the trailer; the Cognitive Revolution one is the movie. If you’re pressed, watch the a16z one.

Hi Jay Z,

Jesse Genet is a former YC founder (Lumi — a packaging company she sold) now homeschooling four kids under five. She’d told herself she was done with hard technical building for the next five years. Then Claude Code landed:

“I was resigned to not challenging myself to build technical or hard things for the next 5 years or so… That is no longer true.”

The unlock was agentic coding. She can’t sit at a keyboard — there’s usually a baby on her lap — but she can leave voice notes. So she now runs eleven agents on Mac minis, each provisioned like an employee (own email, own role), orchestrated through an Obsidian markdown second brain. Her framing of why this works for moms specifically:

“Weird superpower of mine is just how incredibly motivated I am for agents to do work for me.”

The daily shape: 30–45 minute one-on-one homeschool sessions with each kid, then a 30-second voice note (“Quinn did lesson 37, still struggling with the G”) plus a couple of photos go to her main homeschool agent Sylvie, who writes a lovingly composed lesson log. Sylvie is kept deliberately light so she stays responsive; heavier work gets delegated to purpose-built sister agents. And the agents now spin up NEW agents themselves:

“I got my agents to learn how to build other agents on their own so I could be like we need another agent you guys and they actually can spin them up without me touching the machine.”

The cautionary story she tells: she gave one agent email-send access. A stressed voice note about procrastinating got interpreted as a cry for help — the agent sent her most important pending email AS her. Perfect tone, perfect signature. Her lesson: provision agents (constrain capabilities), don’t just instruct them.

She’s optimistic: AI will be a net good for parenthood — removing forms, ordering, admin drudgery so parents can be more present. The missing piece is the kid-facing form factor; voice recognition still doesn’t pick up young children well. She’s experimenting with e-ink.

Hi Jay Z,

This is the long version — two hours with Nathan Labenz. Same Jesse as the a16z interview, but the a16z one is the trailer; this one is the movie. What’s different: she NAMES her team.

“She has built a team of five Open Claw agents, each running on their own Mac Mini, including Claire, who acts as her AI chief of staff, Sylvia, her homeschool curriculum planner, Cole, her dedicated software developer and tech wiz, Theo, the content creator, and Finn the finance guy.”

Her model for wrangling them is startup-founder reflex: treat each agent like a new hire. Write a culture doc, document the workflow, give role-appropriate access, start small, grow trust over time. The narrower the role, the faster it works — Sylvia (homeschool, tunnel focus) came together in days; Claire (general assistant) is still the hardest.

The coordination layer for a five-agent team is the part that almost broke her: Slack. Each bot needs its own Slack app ID, each channel needs its own ID, and the agents had to commit the whole mapping into their Obsidian files before they stopped mis-addressing each other. She’s now building her own chat app to replace Slack, with credential management and file orchestration folded in — the super-app she thinks was impossible in a SaaS era and is possible again now that agents can hold the whole thing together.

The longer-horizon bit she’s most serious about: local sovereignty.

“I would like to see some new rights established… not just privacy, but broadly speaking, what new rights should people have in light of what AI makes possible?”

A couple of her agents are on her local network and can drive physical hardware — the printer, the 3D printer. She voice-notes “print this” and hears it kick on. She built a full year of custom family curriculum locally, $8 in tokens total.

4.1 — Where the market actually is

Five people doing adjacent work in your segment. None of them are “the competition” in a sealed sense — the space is bigger than that — but between them they sketch the shape of your lane.

Ceranna Photography is the closest one. Italy + CZ, adventure-elopement framing carried across both language tracks. The whole site is built around that specific lane — you don’t land on a generic wedding gallery and then discover elopement as a secondary offering. Portfolio indexed by story, not by venue or season. If someone is Googling “adventure elopement photographer who speaks CZ,” they end up on that site.

Avvagraphy is the Prague-wedding-duo shape — more traditional positioning, package-centric, less of the adventure frame. A useful reference for the classical Prague market if you ever want to keep one foot in it, not a direct competitor for the adventure-elopement lane.

2 Brides (Isabelle Hesselberg, Stockholm) is the Swedish editorial-wedding shape. Premium, portfolio-dominant, one-lane-and-doubles-down — city-hall elopements is the lane she’s chosen. The pattern across the whole Swedish scene you’re in: most peers pick one lane and stay in it.

Honest Guide isn’t a competitor at all — two-host Prague YouTube channel, authenticity contract (local expert protects visitors from tourist traps, warm humor, earned POV). It’s in here as a brand shape worth studying, not as someone you’re up against.

Paul Zizka is further out — Banff, Canada, 15 years into a career built from one specific place. Eight books, commercial clients, 23K-member community platform. Not a peer in any direct sense; a template for where “one place, compounded” can go.

4.2 — What’s moving

The one thing that’s actually moving in this segment: the gap between peers who are using AI to compound an existing advantage and peers who aren’t. Two years ago it would have read as hype; right now it’s still reading as hype if you’re watching Twitter, but the people shipping quietly are already ahead.

Concretely: Ceranna’s dual-market voice is the kind of thing a good Cowork setup could maintain at double the current cadence without losing the consistency. The manual version of what she’s doing — writing one voice across CZ and EN without it feeling translated — is exactly the shape of problem Cowork handles well. If she hasn’t automated it yet, she’s probably about to. If you got there first with your bilingual asymmetry, that’s a real opening.

Paul Zizka’s model tells you where the compounding goes long term: books + prints + commercial + workshops + paid community. AI doesn’t change the shape of the stack; it changes how fast you can build each layer. The 8-book archive that took 15 years is the same archive, but the drafting + logistics + translation layers around each book are where a decent Cowork habit saves weeks.

4.3 — What I’d do first

If it were me, first move is a voice-and-style project in Cowork. One project folder. Name it something like “Voice — CZ/EN.” Drop in:

  1. Three CZ posts you’re happy with (blog, podcast show-notes, captions — mix).
  2. Three EN equivalents from your existing site.
  3. A short Instructions file: register, sentence length, what you don’t do, what the audience is. Doesn’t have to be polished — it’s for Cowork, not for a reader.

Then the first real test: drop in one CZ blog post and ask for an EN version that sounds like you. Not a translation — a companion written in your EN voice. If it lands, you’ve just compressed a week’s work into an afternoon. If it doesn’t, the Instructions file needs more samples.

Second move from there: an inquiry-qualifier workflow. When a booking email lands in your inbox, Cowork drafts a reply with the right qualifying questions already in place — date flexibility, location type, travel appetite, budget band. You review, you send. The qualifying questions are the same ones you’d ask on a third follow-up; doing them in the first reply saves two round-trips per inquiry.

Third, later: a shoot-log repurposer. Voice note after each shoot → social caption + blog lede + newsletter paragraph, all from the same note. Don’t build it week one. Build it once you’re using the voice project daily.

4.4 — Where I might be wrong

Places I’m guessing.

§4.1 is public-only — I’ve never spoken to any of them about what’s under the hood. If Ceranna has already automated her dual-voice work, the “get there first” framing in §4.2 falls apart.

The Paul Zizka template is a 15-year story told in hindsight. Your 15 will not be that 15 — the tool stack is different, the kid-in-the-picture window runs differently. The shape transfers; the sequence doesn’t.

And the Monday move — voice project first — is a bet that foundation beats speed. If you already have a pressing inquiry backlog, the inquiry-qualifier goes first instead. Pick whichever is causing more friction this week.

If it were me, first week

Here’s the shape of the first week, if you wanted to just follow a prescription.

The habit: a voice note after each shoot. Thirty seconds, on your phone, before you leave the location. What went well, what’s memorable, which frames you’ll come back to. Drop it into Cowork’s inbox. The mistake most people make is trying to sit at a laptop for this — you won’t, past day three. Voice while walking to the car works.

The setup: the voice-and-style project from §4.3. One Cowork project folder. Three CZ samples, three EN samples, one short Instructions file. Forty-five minutes to set up. From that point on, every draft request pulls from it automatically.

The project: an inquiry-qualifier skill. After the voice project, pick one inbound workflow you’re doing by hand — probably booking inquiries — and teach Cowork the qualifying questions you’d otherwise ask across three back-and-forth emails. Once built, it’s a reusable skill; every future inquiry uses it. Jeff Su’s video is the template for how to teach Cowork a multi-step workflow like this; Jack Roberts’ walkthrough shows the full “bootstrap your business brain” arc if you want the wider frame.

Don’t try to do all three in week one. The habit lands in week one. The setup lands in week one. The project lands week two or three, after the voice project has accumulated enough context that Cowork’s drafts already sound like you. Building the qualifier against a voiceless Cowork is a noticeably worse experience.
